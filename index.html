Artist Statement

I create handcrafted wood artwork in a Nashville two-car garage that hasn't housed a car in years.

Woodworking began as a creative outlet from my full-time career and gradually became my favorite way to spend a quiet weekend. While my husband is out golfing, you'll usually find me in the garage surrounded by hardwood scraps, sawdust, and whatever project has captured my attention that week.

After spending most days in front of a computer screen, woodworking gives me a chance to use a completely different part of my brain. The focus it requires is one of the reasons I keep coming back to the garage every chance I get.

My work focuses primarily on geometric wood mosaics built from individually cut pieces of hardwood. I rarely start with a design — I draw inspiration from the wood itself. An interesting piece of teak, a slab of spalted maple, a pile of scraps with potential. I'm drawn to species that reveal the true character of a tree: figuring, color variation, knots, imperfections, and the story written in the grain.

Most pieces go through multiple iterations before I commit to the glue-down. I move pieces around, change directions, and experiment with combinations until something clicks. The goal is simple: create something interesting to look at and worth sharing with friends and family.

In addition to geometric mosaics, I occasionally incorporate scroll-sawn subjects inspired by nature and everyday life. Whether abstract or representational, every piece is cut, assembled, and finished by hand.

My dad was always a woodworker. I grew up around it but never picked it up myself — until I was approaching my 50th birthday and decided to get serious. I came to woodworking late and largely self-taught. Two years later, I'm still in the garage every chance I get, still learning, and still making sawdust.
